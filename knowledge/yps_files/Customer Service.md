# MISFIRE

You have to be careful with a misfire code only with modern engine controls. Modern ecus monitor the control circuits for the coils and injectors and have specific codes for them when they fail. A random misfire code or a single cylinder misfire code like you have could very easily NOT be the fault of the ecu.

You did not state which component is not receiving a signal from the ecu so we would like to know that information.

Also, could you let us know what parts have been replaced and what diagnostics have been performed?

Modern vehicles have a strategy  to turn the injector off during misfire events to prevent oxygen and fuel being pumped into the catalytic converter and overheating it. This strategy also prevent excessive emissions.

If you are missing the injector signal WITHOUT the presence of an injector circuit code we suggest making sure of the following things.

\-Verify engine mechanical with a compression and leakdown test. A vacuum gauge may also be used to identify mechanical issues.

\-Verify Spark is present IN THE CYLINDER identified by the DTC

\-Verify wiring integrity for the coil and injector with a load test or a voltage drop test preferably. Use ohms testing as a last resort.

These steps will help save you and us time and money. Please email with any questions. Thanks again.

# “GM ABS” PUMP RUNNER

Hello and thanks for contacting us with the issue. Pump Runners are caused by a Transistor on the EBCM circuit board popping. In the case of a “pump runner” the transistor pops the module “open” causing the pump to constantly run even with the engine not running. We replace these on our modules before they go out so we know the module was in working condition prior to shipment. The main cause of the pump runner is a pump that is pulling too much current. The transistor can only handle so much until it pops. You will need to have your mechanic test the “Rush In” amperage at the pump and the “Operating” amperage at the pump. During operation make sure they check for any armatures or fluctuation as well. The other causes could be a wiring issue causing opens or shorts, an issue with the ground strap which are notorious for issues and possibly because the module was installed with the battery hooked up. Make sure the negative terminal is disconnected before install. Another possible issue is the incorrect module was installed. In these situations we offer the buyer one courtesy repair of the module and while the module is being repaired we really need the buyer to have their mechanic review the above possible causes and make repairs as necessary. If you accidentally purchased the wrong part number let us know immediately as we do not want to repair the unit purchased and send it back if it is not a fitment match for your vehicle. # “FORD” THROTTLE BODY RELEARN HARD REBOOT

1\) Disconnect the negative battery terminal.  
2\) Run a jumper wire from the positive battery cable to the now disconnected negative battery cable terminal. NOTE. Do this with the negative cable DISCONNECTED from the battery. The short circuit you’re creating here is designed to drain all power from the capacitors inside the PCM. Leave the jumper connected for about 2-mins.  
3\) Reconnect the negative battery cable to the battery.  
Turn off all electrical accessories before proceeding. Turn off headlights, blower motor, radio, wipers, etc.  
4\) Turn the key to run and wait for all warning lights to turn off. During the period the PCM will command the throttle body plate to sweep from full open to full close to “learn” the new positions.  
5\) Start the engine and let it idle for a full 10-mins until it reaches full operating temperature. During this period the idle may be higher than normal. It should slowly drop as the engine heats up. Watch the engine temp gauge and note when it reaches operating temp. Then continue to let it idle for an additional 5 minutes. It will continue to adjust idle during this period.  
6\) Drive the vehicle at various speed so the PCM learns shift points.
